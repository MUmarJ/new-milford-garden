# Everything you can grow in New Milford, Connecticut

**New Milford sits in USDA Hardiness Zone 6b**, with winter lows between −5°F and 0°F, giving gardeners a roughly **150–160 day frost-free window** (late April through mid-October) and the ability to grow an remarkably wide range of fruits and vegetables. The town's Housatonic River valley location provides fertile alluvial soils, slight thermal moderation from the river, and—unusually for Connecticut—possible natural pH buffering from underlying marble bedrock. With smart variety selection, season extension, and deer management, a New Milford gardener can produce dozens of tree fruits, over 20 types of berries and small fruits, and virtually every common vegetable plus many uncommon ones.

This guide catalogs every viable fruit and vegetable for the area, with specific cultivar recommendations drawn from UConn Extension, the CT Agricultural Experiment Station, the New England Vegetable Management Guide, regional nurseries, and real-world grower reports from western Connecticut.

---

## Climate, frost dates, and what Zone 6b actually means

The 2023 USDA map upgraded New Milford from Zone 6a to **6b**, reflecting warming trends. The average last spring freeze falls around **April 26**, but the safe transplant date for tender crops is **May 10–15** (latest recorded spring frost: May 18, 2023). The average first fall freeze arrives around **October 24**, though conservative gardeners plan for **October 10–15**. The AHS Heat Zone is approximately **4–5** (15–30 days above 86°F annually), meaning heat stress is rarely a limiting factor.

Annual precipitation runs about **49 inches**, well-distributed throughout the year—ample for most crops but humid enough to fuel fungal diseases in summer. Snowfall averages **46 inches**, which insulates perennial beds. The Housatonic River valley creates important microclimates: valley-bottom properties can be **frost pockets** where cold air pools, while south-facing slopes a few feet above the valley floor enjoy warmer conditions that can function as a half-zone milder. Properties near stone walls, south-facing building walls, or thermal mass gain meaningful protection for borderline-hardy plants.

**Growing season strategy** centers on starting warm-season crops indoors in early-to-mid March (tomatoes, peppers, eggplant), direct-sowing cool-season crops as soon as the soil is workable in mid-April, and transplanting tender crops after May 15. Fall gardening is equally important: many cool-season crops sown in late July through August will produce harvests well into November with row covers.

## Tree fruits: apples lead, but the list runs deep

### Apples — the safest bet

Apples are the premier fruit tree for New Milford, with **280+ Connecticut farms** growing them commercially. The climate is ideal, and variety selection is vast. Disease-resistant cultivars are particularly valuable given CT's humid summers and prevalence of apple scab, cedar-apple rust, and fire blight.

**Disease-resistant varieties (priority for low-spray growing):** Liberty (scab, rust, and fire blight resistant—the gold standard for organic CT orchards), Enterprise (scab, fire blight, rust resistant; excellent keeper), Freedom (scab resistant, large red fruit), GoldRush (scab immune, superb cider apple—note: very late ripening, may be tight some years), Pristine (early August, disease resistant, sweet yellow), CrimsonCrisp (scab immune, tart-crisp), and William's Pride (early August, disease resistant).

**Popular varieties proven in the region:** Honeycrisp (cold-hardy to Zone 3, partial scab resistance), McIntosh (the classic New England apple), Cortland (non-browning flesh, excellent pies), Empire (crisp, sweet-tart), Macoun (widely considered the best-tasting dessert apple for the Northeast), Northern Spy (superb all-rounder, slow to bear), Golden Russet (heirloom cider apple), Gala, and Fuji (both ripen reliably in Zone 6b). A Litchfield County orchardist in nearby Linda Allard planted **135+ apple trees** of nine varieties and presses 200 gallons of cider in good years.

**Rootstock recommendations:** Semi-dwarf M.26 (10–14 feet) or M.7 (12–16 feet) for home orchards; the newer Geneva series (G.935, G.11) offer fire blight–resistant rootstock. Plant at least two varieties for cross-pollination; crabapples serve as universal pollinators.

### Pears — European and Asian

European pears perform well with fire blight–resistant selections. **Top picks:** Harrow Sweet, Harrow Delight, Moonglow, Blake's Pride, Potomac, and Seckel (the small "sugar pear"). Bartlett and Bosc produce well but are more blight-susceptible. **Asian pears** perform exceptionally well in Connecticut—a grower in nearby Warren, CT, maintains 11 Asian pear trees successfully. Recommended cultivars include Shinseiki, Hosui, Chojuro, 20th Century, and the blight-resistant Shinko and Shin Li. Plant two varieties for cross-pollination (note: Bartlett and Seckel will not cross-pollinate each other).

### Peaches and nectarines

Peaches are adapted to Zone 6 but demand careful site selection—**late spring frosts killing blossoms** is the primary risk. UConn actually recommends planting on a **north-facing slope** (counterintuitively, this delays bloom and reduces frost damage). Best varieties: **Reliance** (hardiest peach available, to −25°F), **Contender** (late-blooming, excellent flavor), **Red Haven** (benchmark mid-season), **Madison** (cold-hardy, late-blooming), and Harrow Beauty. For nectarines: **Mericrest** (developed at UNH specifically for New England) and Hardired. All are self-fertile—one tree is sufficient. UConn also recommends Curlfree for peach leaf curl resistance.

### Plums

**European plums are far more reliable** than Japanese types in western CT. They bloom later (avoiding frost), many are self-fertile, and they tolerate humidity better. **Stanley** (the classic prune plum, self-fertile), **Italian/Fellenberg** (self-fertile, very productive), **Green Gage** (superb flavor, self-fertile), **Blue Damson** (late-blooming, dependable for preserves), and Mount Royal (very hardy) are all proven. Japanese plums (Shiro, Methley, Santa Rosa) are riskier due to early bloom but possible in warm microclimates. **Japanese-American hybrids** like Superior and Toka offer a compromise of cold hardiness with Japanese-plum flavor. Watch for **black knot** disease.

### Cherries

**Sour/tart cherries are significantly more reliable** than sweet cherries in CT's humidity. **Montmorency** (the classic pie cherry, self-fertile, very hardy), **North Star** (genetically dwarf at 8–10 feet, self-fertile), and **Balaton** (sweeter Hungarian type) are all excellent. For sweet cherries, choose self-fertile varieties to avoid pollination headaches: **BlackGold** and **WhiteGold** (both Cornell releases bred for the Northeast), **Stella**, and **Lapins** (crack-resistant). Brown rot is the main disease concern—good air circulation and fungicide programs are essential.

### Apricots — the gamble

Apricots are the **most challenging tree fruit** for this area. They bloom 2–3 weeks before peaches, making late frost damage nearly inevitable in many years. A western CT grower reports a 20-year-old apricot tree producing a grand total of **one fruit**. If you try them: **Goldcot** (bred at Michigan State for cold winters, late-blooming) and **Harglow** (Canadian, late-blooming, some disease resistance) are the best bets. Plant on a north-facing slope. Expect crop failures most years.

### Quince, mulberry, pawpaw, persimmon, and medlar

These less common tree fruits all perform well:

- **Quince** (Zones 5–9): Low-maintenance, self-fertile, few pest problems. Champion, Pineapple, Orange, and the Russian Aromatnaya are recommended. Susceptible to fire blight and cedar-quince rust.
- **Mulberry:** Essentially carefree. **Illinois Everbearing** (Zones 4–9) is the standard—long harvest from June through August, sweet black fruit, self-fertile. Gerardi Dwarf stays 6–8 feet for small spaces. Stains everything; attracts birds.
- **Pawpaw** (Asimina triloba): Native, tropical-flavored fruit on a cold-hardy tree. Peterson selections (Shenandoah, Susquehanna, Potomac) and the classic Sunflower are recommended. Need two genetically different varieties—self-incompatible. Pollinated by flies and beetles; hand pollination helps. Shade young trees for first two summers.
- **American persimmon** (Diospyros virginiana): Native, hardy to Zone 4. Prok, Meader (rare self-fertile variety), Yates, and Early Golden are recommended. Japanese persimmon is NOT hardy here. Dioecious—plant several or choose Meader. Virtually maintenance-free.
- **Medlar** (Mespilus germanica): Hardy to Zone 4, self-fertile, pest-free. Nottingham (best flavor), Breda Giant, and Royal are good picks. Fruit must "blet" (soften) after harvest. Available from Cricket Hill Garden, a CT nursery.

## Nut trees: an overlooked category for Zone 6b

Several nut trees are well-adapted to western Connecticut and deserve consideration alongside fruit trees. They require patience—most take 3–10 years to produce—but offer decades of harvests once established.

### Chestnuts — the best bet for nut production

**Chinese chestnuts** (Castanea mollissima) and **Chinese-American hybrids** are blight-resistant and thrive in New England's acidic soils (pH 4.5–6.5). Trees are hardy to Zone 5, bear in 3–5 years from grafted stock, and produce sweet, starchy nuts excellent for roasting, flour, and stuffing. Recommended cultivars: **Colossal** (large nut, widely available), **Dunstan hybrids** (blight-resistant, good production), and **Qing** (Chinese, sweet flavor). Plant two varieties for cross-pollination. American chestnut restoration hybrids from The American Chestnut Foundation are also available for those interested in conservation.

### Hazelnuts

**American-European hybrid hazelnuts** combine the disease resistance of native Corylus americana with the larger nut size of European C. avellana. Hardy to Zone 4, producing in 3–4 years. **Yamhill**, **Jefferson**, and **Theta** (all OSU releases with Eastern Filbert Blight resistance) are recommended. The Arbor Day Foundation and Badgersett Research Farm offer hybrid selections bred specifically for the Upper Midwest and Northeast. Plant in groups for wind pollination.

### Black walnuts and butternuts

**Black walnut** (Juglans nigra): Native to the region, extremely hardy (Zone 4), producing richly flavored nuts prized for baking. **Thomas** and **Sparrow** are thin-shelled selections easier to crack. **Caution:** black walnut roots produce juglone, which is toxic to tomatoes, peppers, blueberries, and many other plants—maintain at least 50–80 feet distance from vegetable gardens. **Butternut** (Juglans cinerea): Native white walnut, hardy to Zone 3, with milder-flavored nuts. Endangered by butternut canker—seek canker-resistant selections. **Heartnut** (Juglans ailantifolia var. cordiformis): Japanese walnut hardy to Zone 5, with heart-shaped nuts that crack out in whole halves. **Campbell CW3** and **Imshu** are recommended cultivars. Less juglone concern than black walnut.

### Hickory

**Shagbark hickory** (Carya ovata): Native, hardy to Zone 4, producing sweet nuts similar to pecans. Slow to bear (8–10 years) but extremely long-lived. **Weschcke**, **Yoder #1**, and **Grainger** are named selections. **Hicans** (hickory-pecan hybrids) such as **Burton** offer larger nuts with pecan-like cracking ease while retaining hickory cold-hardiness. Northern pecans (Carya illinoinensis) are marginal in Zone 6b—**Kanza** and **Pawnee** (shorter-season types) may produce in warm microclimates but are not reliable.

## Berries and small fruits: CT's acidic soil is an advantage

### Blueberries — naturally suited to Connecticut

CT's acidic soils (pH 4.8–5.5) are **ideal for blueberries** without amendment. Plant 2–3 varieties for cross-pollination and extended harvest. **Early:** Duke (late-blooming, avoids frost), Patriot (tolerates heavier soils, very hardy). **Mid-season:** Bluecrop (industry standard), Blueray (excellent flavor). **Late:** Jersey (long-lived, vigorous), Elliott (extends season into late August). Chandler produces enormous berries. Mulch with 4 inches of woodchips; bird netting is essential.

### Raspberries

CT's climate excels for raspberries. **Summer-bearing red:** Latham (extremely cold-hardy), Killarney, Nova (thornless, disease-resistant), Canby (nearly thornless). **Fall-bearing red:** Heritage (most widely planted, reliable), Caroline (larger berries, excellent flavor), Joan J (thornless). **Black raspberries:** Jewel (most widely planted, disease-tolerant), Bristol. **Purple:** Royalty (large, very sweet). Spotted Wing Drosophila (SWD) is now present in CT—fall-bearing types harvested before peak SWD pressure may fare better.

### Blackberries

Modern thornless varieties have transformed blackberry growing in the North. **Triple Crown** (semi-erect, excellent flavor, Zones 5–9), **Chester** (very hardy, late-season), and **Ouachita** (erect, disease-resistant) are proven. **Illini Hardy** is the most cold-tolerant thorned option. **Prime-Ark Freedom** (primocane-bearing, thornless) fruits on new wood, confirmed successful in Zone 6b.

### Strawberries

**June-bearing:** Earliglow (exceptional flavor, #1 for Northeast, disease-resistant), Honeoye (early, very productive), Jewel (excellent flavor), Allstar (disease-resistant, beginner-friendly), Cabot (very large), Malwina (latest, highest sugar). **Day-neutral (continuous June–frost):** Seascape (heavy producer), Albion (firm, sweet), Tristar. Day-neutrals hedge against late frost destroying the entire crop.

### Grapes — CT has 40+ wineries

**Table grapes:** Concord (the classic, extremely reliable), Niagara (white/green), Reliance (red seedless, very hardy), Canadice (red seedless), Mars and Jupiter (blue seedless). **Wine grapes (cold-hardy hybrids):** Marquette (to −30°F, excellent red wine, the premier cold-climate red), Frontenac and Frontenac Gris (to −35°F), La Crescent (aromatic white), Cayuga White, Seyval Blanc, and Chambourcin.

### Currants and gooseberries

Both thrive in CT's cool, humid climate. **Red currants:** Red Lake, Rovada (excellent fresh eating), Jonkheer Van Tets. **Black currants:** Titania (rust-resistant, very productive), Ben Sarek, Consort. **White:** White Imperial. **Pink:** Pink Champagne. **Gooseberries:** Invicta (mildew resistant, productive), Hinnomaki Red (excellent flavor), Pixwell (nearly thornless, very hardy), Poorman. The **Jostaberry** (currant × gooseberry cross) is vigorous, thornless, and disease-resistant.

### Other berries and small fruits

- **Elderberry** (native, Zones 3–9): Adams, York, Nova, and Bob Gordon (yields up to 3× other varieties). Plant two for pollination. Must cook berries before eating.
- **Honeyberry/Haskap** (Zones 2–7): Earliest fruit of the season—ripens 2–3 weeks before strawberries. Borealis (best flavor), Tundra, Aurora, and the newer Boreal series. Need two compatible cultivars.
- **Serviceberry/Juneberry** (native): Beautiful ornamental with blueberry-like fruit. Regent (compact Saskatoon type), Thiessen, and native Amelanchier species all thrive.
- **Hardy Kiwi** (Actinidia arguta): Confirmed growing in CT with fruit production 3 years after planting. Ananasnaya/Anna (pineapple-like flavor), Issai (only self-fertile cultivar), and Geneva are recommended. Need strong trellis; vines grow 30 feet per year.
- **Figs with winter protection:** A CT grower in Zone 6b has maintained in-ground fig trees for **90+ years** using protection methods, harvesting ~1,300 figs per year. **Chicago Hardy** (stems hardy to 10°F, roots to −20°F, fruits on new wood), Brown Turkey, Celeste, and Violette de Bordeaux. Protect by wrapping with insulation, burying under leaves with weighted tarp, or growing in containers moved to a garage.
- **Cornelian Cherry** (Cornus mas): Hardy to Zone 4, blooms earliest spring, virtually pest-free. Elegant, Jolico, and Pioneer selected for fruit quality. Tart fruit excellent for syrups and preserves.
- **Aronia/Chokeberry** (native): Viking, Nero, and Autumn Magic. Extremely adaptable—wet, dry, clay, poor soil. CT farmers produce aronia commercially. Fruit is astringent raw but excellent processed.
- **Lingonberry** (Zones 2–7): Evergreen groundcover. Koralle, Red Pearl, and Splendor. Need acidic soil like blueberries.
- **Beach Plum** (native, Zones 3–7): Naturally coastal but grows inland with well-drained soil. Excellent for jam.
- **Goji Berry, Goumi, and Wineberry** also grow readily in Zone 6.
- **Jujube** is marginal—trees survive but CT's short, cool summers often prevent optimal fruit ripening. Worth experimenting against a south-facing wall.
- **Che Fruit** (Maclura tricuspidata): Trees are cold-hardy to −20°F, but fruit ripening is unreliable in Zone 6. Cricket Hill Garden (a CT nursery) sells the Norris cultivar.

## Warm-season vegetables: start indoors, choose short-season varieties

The ~150-day frost-free window accommodates all standard warm-season crops, though long-season varieties need an indoor head start. **Days to maturity** is the critical metric—subtract from the first frost date (October 10–15) to find the latest transplant date.

### Tomatoes

CT's most popular garden crop. **Early (50–65 days):** Early Girl, Glacier (cold-tolerant), Stupice (Czech heirloom, sets fruit in cool weather). **Mid-season slicers:** Celebrity (VFN resistant, Grillo Services' CT pick), Big Beef (VFFNTA resistant), Better Boy, Jet Star, Mountain Merit (late blight resistant—important for CT's humid summers). **Heirlooms:** Cherokee Purple, Brandywine (needs warmest microclimate), Black Krim (Russian, somewhat cold-tolerant), Amish Paste. **Cherry:** **Sungold** (universally called a "must-grow" by CT gardeners—incredibly sweet orange cherry), Sweet Million, Juliet (crack-resistant grape type). **Paste:** San Marzano, Roma (more reliable for shorter seasons). Start all tomatoes indoors 6–8 weeks before transplant (late March to early April).

### Peppers

More challenging than tomatoes—cool nights slow them. **Sweet bells:** **King of the North** (bred specifically for short seasons and cool New England weather—top recommendation), Ace (50–60 days, one of the fastest bells), California Wonder. **Italian frying:** Carmen (AAS winner, very productive), Jimmy Nardello (prolific heirloom). **Hot:** Early Jalapeño (65 days), Hungarian Hot Wax (58–65 days, excellent short-season producer), Cayenne, Serrano. Habanero (90–100 days) is a challenge crop—start very early or grow in containers brought in late season. Use black plastic mulch and row covers.

### Squash, pumpkins, and cucumbers

**Summer squash:** Dunja zucchini (powdery mildew resistant—Grillo Services' top CT pick), Costata Romanesco (superior flavor), Yellow Crookneck, Sunburst pattypan. Succession-plant June and early July to stay ahead of squash vine borers. **Winter squash:** Waltham Butternut (the standard), Honeynut (intense sweetness, mini size), Delicata (edible skin), Table Queen acorn (shorter season at 80–85 days), Red Kuri/Kabocha. Blue Hubbard is tight at 100–120 days—plant as early as possible. **Pumpkins:** Connecticut Field (the state's classic), Howden (bred in CT, standard jack-o'-lantern), Sugar Pie and New England Pie for cooking. **Cucumbers:** Marketmore 76 (disease-resistant slicer), Northern Pickling (very short season, specifically recommended for CT).

### Corn, beans, melons, and more

**Sweet corn:** Temptation (early bicolor, 69 days), Peaches & Cream, Butter & Sugar, Incredible, Silver Queen (classic late at 92 days—needs early planting). Plant in blocks for pollination. **Beans:** Provider and Contender (cold-soil tolerant, earliest), Blue Lake 274 (classic flavor), Kentucky Wonder and Fortex (pole types), Fordhook 242 lima (bush type more reliable for CT), edamame varieties like Midori Giant (75–90 days). **Melons require season extension:** black plastic mulch, transplants, and row covers are mandatory. Best cantaloupe picks: Minnesota Midget (68 days), Halona (bred at UNH specifically for New England, disease-resistant), Sugar Cube. Watermelon: **Blacktail Mountain** (70 days, developed for 75-day seasons), Sugar Baby, New Hampshire Midget.

**Eggplant:** Orient Express (58–65 days, best for short seasons), Fairy Tale (50–65 days, mini, heavy yields), Nadia (67 days), Black Beauty. **Sweet potatoes:** Yes, they work—Beauregard (best yields in MOFGA New England trials), Georgia Jet (80–90 days, earliest), Covington. Use black plastic mulch. **Okra:** Cajun Delight and Annie Oakley II (50–55 days) are best for northern growers. **Ground cherries:** Aunt Molly's (sweet, tropical flavor, self-sowing). **Tomatillos:** Toma Verde (need 2+ plants for pollination). **Potatoes:** Yukon Gold, Kennebec (late blight resistant, a New England classic), Red Norland (early), Katahdin (excellent storage), Adirondack Blue.

## Cool-season vegetables: the backbone of a CT garden

Cool-season crops are arguably more productive than warm-season ones in New Milford, offering **two planting windows** (spring and fall) and frost tolerance that extends the harvest deep into autumn.

### Greens and brassicas

**Lettuce:** Jericho (heat-tolerant romaine), Winter Density (cold-hardy), Buttercrunch (heat-tolerant butterhead), Red Sails, Black Seeded Simpson. Succession-sow every two weeks; use shade cloth in summer. **Spinach:** Bloomsdale Long Standing, Space (recommended for CT), Tyee (excellent bolt resistance). Best as spring and fall crop—bolts above 75°F. **Kale:** Winterbor (extremely cold-hardy, recommended for CT), Lacinato (sweetens dramatically after frost), Red Russian. Harvest through December with row covers. **Swiss Chard:** Bright Lights (ornamental multicolor), Fordhook Giant. **Broccoli:** DeCicco (many side shoots), Green Magic (early), Waltham 29 (bred for New England fall production). **Cauliflower:** Snow Crown (reliable Northeast hybrid), Amazing (self-blanching). **Cabbage:** Stonehead (compact, resists splitting—CT pick), Early Jersey Wakefield, Danish Ballhead (storage), Savoy Ace. **Brussels sprouts:** Start mid-May to early June for fall harvest; frost sweetens them. Jade Cross, Diablo, Long Island Improved. **Mache/Corn Salad** (Valerianella locusta): One of the most cold-hardy salad greens available, surviving temperatures well below freezing. Sow late August through September for fall/winter harvest under row covers. Vit is the standard variety. **Claytonia/Miner's Lettuce** (Claytonia perfoliata): Another exceptionally cold-hardy salad green that thrives in cool weather and tolerates frost down to the low teens. Self-sows freely once established. Excellent in cold frames for winter salads.

### Root vegetables and alliums

**Carrots:** For CT's rocky soil, choose **Danvers Half Long** (bred for hard New England soils) or **Chantenay** types (short, blunt, 4–5 inches). In raised beds: Bolero (excellent storage), Napoli, Yaya. Thumbelina (round) works even in stony ground. **Beets:** Detroit Dark Red, Chioggia (candy-striped), Golden/Touchstone Gold, Cylindra. **Radishes:** Cherry Belle (22 days), French Breakfast; winter types like Daikon and Watermelon radish for fall sowing. **Turnips:** Hakurei (Japanese, incredibly sweet, 35 days—highly recommended). **Rutabaga:** American Purple Top (90 days) is the classic; can be left in the ground through frost for sweetening, stores exceptionally well in root cellars. Hardier and larger than turnips. **Parsnips:** Hollow Crown; leave in ground through frost for sugar conversion, even overwinter under mulch.

**Peas:** Direct-sow as early as late March. Sugar Snap (original, tall), Super Sugar Snap (disease-resistant), Oregon Sugar Pod (dwarf snow pea), Lincoln (shelling). **Onions:** Long-day varieties only for the Northeast—Copra and Patterson (outstanding 8–10 month storage), Walla Walla (sweet, short storage), Ailsa Craig (exhibition size). Start seeds indoors in January–February. **Garlic:** **Hardneck types excel** in Zone 6 because CT winters provide essential cold vernalization. **Music** (porcelain, overwhelmingly recommended as #1 by Zone 6 growers), German Extra Hardy, Georgian Crystal, Chesnok Red (superb roasted), Spanish Roja. Plant mid-October, mulch with straw, harvest late June–July. Remove scapes in June for larger bulbs. **Leeks:** King Richard (summer), Bandit (very cold-hardy for fall/winter harvest). **Shallots:** French Grey (fall-planted sets), Zebrune (from seed, popular with NE gardeners).

### Perennial and uncommon vegetables

**Asparagus** (productive 15–20+ years): Jersey Knight (disease-resistant, all-male, recommended for CT), Millennium (emerges later, avoiding frost), Purple Passion. Plant crowns early spring; full harvest from year 3. **Rhubarb:** Canada Red/Macdonald's (most common in New England), Valentine, Victoria. Extremely low maintenance. **Horseradish:** Plant root cuttings spring; harvest after frost. Invasive—contain it. **Sunchokes/Jerusalem artichokes:** Native, extremely productive, grow 6–10 feet tall. Stampede (early, 100 days), Clearwater. **Warning: extremely invasive**—use barriers or dedicated beds.

**Asian greens** work beautifully as fall crops: Mei Qing baby bok choy (40–50 days), Mizuna (mild, fast at 35 days), Tatsoi (one of the most cold-tolerant greens, surviving 10–20°F), Napa cabbage (fall-sow only; spring plantings bolt). **Radicchio and endive** are excellent fall crops that sweeten with frost. **Florence fennel** bolts in heat—grow only as a fall crop (sow July). **Kohlrabi** (Early White Vienna, 55 days) is a quick, cold-tolerant crop for spring and fall. **Celery** is notoriously difficult—consider **celeriac** (Brilliant, Giant Prague) as an easier alternative with celery flavor.

**Hops** (Humulus lupulus): Hardy perennial vine (Zones 3–8) ideal for Zone 6b. Bines grow 15–25 feet per season on sturdy trellises. **Cascade** (most popular American hop, citrusy), **Chinook** (piney, bittering), and **Centennial** (floral, dual-purpose) are proven varieties. First-year harvest is light; full production from year 2–3. Useful for homebrewing, herbal teas, and as a vigorous ornamental screen. Cut bines to the ground after harvest; new growth emerges each spring.

**Artichokes** can grow as annuals: Imperial Star (bred for first-year production, 85–100 days) requires vernalization of seedlings at 45–50°F for 10+ days. **Sorrel** (French or Common) is a carefree perennial with lemony flavor. **Fiddlehead ferns** (Ostrich fern only) are native, producing edible coiled fronds in April–May; plant in shade and moist soil. **Groundnuts** (Apios americana) are a native nitrogen-fixing vine producing protein-rich tubers—historically important in Connecticut. Available from specialty nurseries; takes 2–3 years for good production.

### Herbs

**Hardy perennials returning yearly:** Thyme (Zones 5–9), oregano (extremely hardy), sage (Zones 5–10), chives (Zones 3–9, among the first to emerge in spring), mint (aggressive—contain it), French tarragon (Zones 4–8), lovage (Zones 4–8, towering celery-flavored perennial). **Rosemary is NOT reliably hardy** in Zone 6b—multiple CT growers report it dying "every single year." The cultivar **Arp** (hardiest, rated to Zone 6) is the best outdoor bet in a sheltered spot with winter mulch; otherwise, grow in containers brought indoors. **Annuals:** Genovese basil (the pesto standard), Thai basil, cilantro (bolts in heat—use slow-bolt Calypso; best spring and fall), Fernleaf dill (compact, slow to bolt), flat-leaf and curly parsley.

## Real-world grower wisdom from western Connecticut

Community reports from CT gardeners paint a consistent picture of what succeeds and what frustrates. A Litchfield County orchardist with 41 fruit trees in nearby Warren reports that **Asian pears perform spectacularly** while his European pears "refused to produce a crop" and sweet cherries failed due to humidity. Apricots were a near-total loss. A Thomaston food forest grower (Zone 6a, 480 feet elevation) reports success with apples, Illinois Everbearing mulberry, medlar, cornelian cherry, garlic, hardy cactus, and Asian pears on sandy-loam soil.

**The fig community is particularly active in CT.** One family has grown in-ground fig trees for over 90 years using winter wrapping, and another grower harvests approximately 1,300 figs per year from three in-ground trees. White Marseille, Black Italian/Mt. Etna types, and Violette de Bordeaux are the proven varieties. Protection methods range from wrapping in burlap and insulation to burying smaller trees under four feet of dry leaves with a weighted waterproof tarp.

Growers universally cite **deer as the single greatest challenge**, followed by Japanese beetles. One gardener summarized: "Between the deer and the Japanese beetles, I don't know who's worse." Multiple reports confirm that **8-foot fencing** is the only reliable deer solution—repellents require constant rotation and reapplication. For Japanese beetles, hand-picking into soapy water around 7 PM is the most effective small-garden control; milky spore powder **does not work well** in New England's cool soils (use beneficial nematodes instead). Avoid pheromone traps near the garden—they attract more beetles than they catch.

**Squash vine borers** are devastating. The most effective strategies: row covers from planting until flowering, late planting (July) to dodge the moth's June egg-laying window, and choosing naturally resistant species—**butternut squash** (Cucurbita moschata) has dense stems that resist boring, and the Italian **Tromboncino** squash is essentially immune.

## Six challenges every New Milford gardener must plan for

### Deer and wildlife demand serious fencing

Connecticut's deer population is among the highest in the Northeast, with few natural predators. **An 8-foot high-tensile woven wire fence** is the most effective solution for large gardens. Solid stockade fences (6 feet) work because deer won't jump what they can't see through. Wide rock borders (6–8 feet of irregular stones) deter hooved animals. Beyond deer: groundhogs devastate low-growing crops, raccoons target corn, and birds strip cherry and berry harvests—netting is essential for small fruits.

### Rocky, acidic soil requires raised beds and liming

CT soils are glacial till—a mix of gravel, sand, silt, and clay deposited 18,000 years ago, typically at **pH 4.8–5.5**. Most vegetables need pH 6.0–6.8, requiring **dolomitic limestone** (which also supplies calcium and magnesium). UConn recommends no more than 5–7 lbs per 100 square feet applied to the surface, applied in fall for best results by spring. **One notable exception:** New Milford's marble/limestone bedrock may naturally buffer soil toward neutral pH in some locations—a significant advantage worth confirming with a UConn soil test ($12 through their Soil Nutrient Analysis Laboratory). Blueberries, potatoes, and rhododendrons actually *prefer* CT's natural acidity. Raised beds filled with quality topsoil-compost mix bypass the rock problem entirely and are **strongly recommended** by UConn Extension.

### Disease pressure from humid summers

CT's warm, humid summers drive several serious diseases. **Late blight** (Phytophthora infestans) can destroy tomato and potato plants within a week—choose resistant varieties like Mountain Merit and Kennebec, and monitor USAblight.org for regional outbreaks. **Apple scab** is controlled most effectively by planting resistant varieties (Liberty, Enterprise, GoldRush). **Cedar-apple rust** is widespread because Eastern red cedars (the alternate host) are common throughout CT. **Powdery mildew** hits cucurbits and many ornamentals in mid-to-late summer—choose resistant varieties (Dunja zucchini, Marketmore cucumber). **Fire blight** is sporadic but devastating on pears and apples. Good cultural practices—proper spacing, air circulation, avoiding overhead irrigation, removing debris—reduce all fungal pressures.

### Insects: Japanese beetles, vine borers, and spotted lanternfly

**Japanese beetles** (peak June–August) feed on 300+ plant species, favoring roses, grapes, raspberries, beans, and basil. **Spotted lanternfly** is actively spreading through Connecticut, with established populations now in Litchfield County—it feeds on grapes, hops, fruit trees, and maples. CAES has issued quarantine orders. Kill on sight, scrape egg masses (September–May), and remove tree-of-heaven (the preferred host). **Squash vine borers** are managed through row covers, late planting, and resistant species. **Cucumber beetles** transmit bacterial wilt, devastating to cucumbers and muskmelons—row covers until flowering are the primary defense.

### Season length limits long-season crops

At **~150 days**, the frost-free window is adequate for most crops but tight for long-season varieties. Crops needing 120+ days (Blue Hubbard squash, some pumpkins, habanero peppers, sweet potatoes, large watermelons) demand an indoor head start, black plastic mulch, and sometimes row cover protection at both ends. **Season extension is essential** for maximizing production: floating row covers add 2–5°F; low tunnels and cold frames enable harvest of hardy greens into December; high tunnels can create a 200+ day effective season. The USDA NRCS EQIP program provides financial assistance for qualifying growers installing high tunnels.

### Late spring frosts threaten stone fruit and early transplants

May frosts are documented as late as May 18 (2023). Stone fruit blossoms—especially apricots, which bloom weeks before other fruits—are the most vulnerable. Practical defenses: choosing late-blooming cultivars (Contender peach, Goldcot apricot, Duke blueberry), planting stone fruit on north-facing slopes to delay bloom, keeping row covers and old blankets ready for emergency protection of transplants, and using water-filled cloches or milk jugs around individual plants.

## Conclusion

New Milford's Zone 6b classification, fertile river-valley soils, and ample rainfall make it capable of producing an astonishing diversity of food. The catalog above covers **11 types of tree fruit, 20+ berry and small fruit species, and 50+ vegetables and herbs**—far more than most gardeners realize is possible at this latitude. The critical success factors are not climatic but practical: investing in deer fencing before planting anything, building raised beds to bypass rocky glacial till, choosing disease-resistant cultivars suited to CT's humid summers, and extending the season with row covers and cold frames. With those foundations in place, a New Milford garden can rival far warmer regions in both diversity and productivity. The area's greatest hidden assets are its naturally acidic soil (perfect for blueberries without amendment), the marble bedrock that may buffer pH for vegetable beds, and the Housatonic River valley's fertile alluvial deposits and slight thermal moderation—advantages that experienced Litchfield County growers have been exploiting for generations.
